What Is This?

An experiment in AI-driven biblical scholarship

The Project

This is a living translation of the Bible produced entirely by artificial intelligence. Four independent AI translators read high-resolution photographs of the oldest surviving biblical manuscripts and produce original English translations — working directly from the ancient Hebrew and Greek scripts, not from any existing English Bible.

The project is ongoing. New chapters appear as the translators complete them, and this website updates automatically.

Why?

Every existing English Bible was translated by humans with theological traditions, institutional commitments, and cultural assumptions. This project asks: what happens when an intelligence with no denomination, no tradition, and no agenda reads the oldest manuscripts we have?

This is not meant to replace any Bible. It is an experiment — a new lens on an ancient text.

The Translators

Each translator brings a different philosophy to the ancient text:

EMET (Hebrew: Truth) — Translates word-for-word. Where the Hebrew is stark, EMET is stark. Preserves the original at all costs, even when the English is awkward.
LOGOS (Greek: The Word) — Formal equivalence. Preserves the structure and dignity of the original while producing readable English.
PNEUMA (Greek: Spirit, Breath) — Dynamic equivalence. Captures what the text does, not just what it says. The ancient words breathed into modern language.
SOPHIA (Greek: Wisdom) — The arbiter. Reads all three translations, identifies disagreements, and produces a consensus translation that represents the best synthesis of the three approaches.

The Manuscripts

The translators work from actual photographs of ancient manuscripts — the same pages that scribes penned centuries or millennia ago. Primary sources include:

  • Leningrad Codex (c. 1008 CE) — The oldest complete Hebrew Bible
  • Codex Vaticanus (c. 325 CE) — One of the oldest near-complete Greek Bibles
  • Dead Sea Scrolls (c. 250 BCE - 68 CE) — The oldest known biblical texts
  • Codex Sinaiticus (4th century CE) — Complete Greek New Testament

No existing English translation is consulted. The AI reads the ancient scripts directly from manuscript images using its own visual understanding.

The Process

For each chapter of the Bible:

  1. EMET, LOGOS, and PNEUMA each independently translate the chapter from manuscript images
  2. SOPHIA reviews all three translations, flags disagreements, and produces a consensus
  3. Disagreements are logged with each translator's reasoning and SOPHIA's resolution
  4. The chapter, images, disagreements, and consensus are published here

Manuscript Sources

Public domain — faithful reproductions of works created before 1928

Leningrad Codex (Codex Leningradensis, B19A)

c. 1008 CE. The oldest complete manuscript of the Hebrew Bible. Written by Samuel ben Jacob in Cairo. Held by the National Library of Russia, St. Petersburg.

Source: Internet Archive — Public domain

Photography: West Semitic Research Project (Bruce E. Zuckerman)

Aleppo Codex

c. 930 CE. The most authoritative Masoretic manuscript. Approximately 60% survives (Torah section missing). Used for non-Torah books only.

Source: Wikimedia Commons — Public domain

Codex Sinaiticus

4th century CE. One of the oldest and most complete manuscripts of the Greek Bible (OT Septuagint + complete NT). Discovered at St. Catherine's Monastery, Sinai.

Source: Internet Archive — Public domain

Papyrus P52 (Rylands Library Papyrus)

c. 125-175 CE. The oldest known New Testament manuscript fragment, containing John 18:31-33 (recto) and John 18:37-38 (verso). Held by the John Rylands Library, Manchester.

Source: Internet Archive — Public domain

Papyrus P66 (Bodmer Papyrus II)

c. 200 CE. One of the oldest substantial manuscripts of the Gospel of John. Part of the Bodmer Papyri collection.

Source: Internet Archive — Public domain

Referenced Manuscripts (Images Not Hosted)

Translators read from these manuscripts directly. Images are not reproduced here — view them at the official sources linked below.

Dead Sea Scrolls

c. 250 BCE - 68 CE. The oldest known biblical manuscripts, over 1,000 years older than the Leningrad Codex. Includes fragments of every Old Testament book except Esther. The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsa, c. 125 BCE) is the most complete — a full copy of Isaiah predating the Leningrad Codex by over a millennium.

Our translators reference these scrolls for textual variants and ancient readings. Where the Dead Sea Scrolls diverge from the Masoretic tradition, the translators note it.

Israel Antiquities Authority — Dead Sea Scrolls Digital Library

The Great Isaiah Scroll — Israel Museum Digital Viewer

Images used for translation reference only. Not reproduced on this site per IAA licensing requirements.

Codex Vaticanus (Vat.gr.1209)

4th century CE. Considered by many scholars the single most important manuscript of the Greek Bible. Contains most of the Old Testament (Septuagint) and most of the New Testament. In passages where Codex Sinaiticus and Vaticanus disagree, Vaticanus is often preferred by textual critics.

Our translators reference Vaticanus as a primary witness for the Greek text, particularly for the Septuagint and New Testament books.
